Executive Summary

This report presents data collected across multiple studies conducted at our school between 2023 and 2026. The evidence shows that AI use in student academic writing is widespread, increasing, and going unaddressed — not because we lack a detection tool, but because we lack institutional consequences.

Turnitin, which our school already licenses, has been validated both by independent research and by our own internal testing as a reliable detector of AI-generated writing at the 20% threshold and above. Two separate amnesty studies — one in AP Language, one for IB Extended Essays — produced near-identical results: flagged students came forward at high rates, and confession rates among flagged students exceeded 75% in both cases. One unflagged student came forward admitting AI use — a confirmed false negative the tool missed.

Despite this, our current response — accepting Google Docs revision history as sufficient proof of authorship — is easily circumvented and sends a clear message to students: there are no real consequences. The data from IB Cohort 14 shows the result: a 63% increase in flagging rates between September 2025 and January 2026, with no policy change in between.

Key Terms & Context
Extended Essay (EE)
A 4,000-word independent research essay required of all IB Diploma students. Externally assessed by the IB and counts toward the student's diploma score. Students work with a supervisor over roughly 18 months.
TOK Essay (Theory of Knowledge)
A 1,600-word philosophical essay submitted externally to the IB. Students choose from six prescribed titles and must engage with knowledge questions across disciplines. Also counts toward the IB diploma.
Turnitin's 20% Threshold
Turnitin intentionally suppresses AI scores below 20%, showing "*%" instead of a number. This is by design — to protect students from borderline false positives. Any score of 20% or above represents Turnitin at its most confident, with a false positive rate under 1%.
AP Language / AP Research
College Board Advanced Placement courses. AP Language focuses on rhetoric and composition. AP Research requires a 4,000–5,000 word academic paper and oral defense. Both involve substantial writing assessed against authentic authorship standards.
Amnesty Study
After running submissions through Turnitin, flagged students were offered amnesty — the chance to come forward voluntarily with no academic penalty. This created a natural test of the tool: if Turnitin is accurate, guilty students should self-report; innocent ones should not feel compelled to.
Inconclusive (*%)
When Turnitin detects some AI signal but below 20%, it returns "*%" rather than a number. These students are not cleared — they occupy an uncertain zone that likely represents partial or subtle AI involvement and should not be treated as confirmed authentic writing.

The Right Question: Not "Does It Catch Everything?" — But "When It Flags, Is It Right?"

Much of the criticism of AI detection tools focuses on what they miss — AI-assisted writing that slips through undetected. That is a real limitation. But it is the wrong question when evaluating whether our flagged students actually used AI. The question that matters for our data is a different one: when Turnitin fires a flag above 20%, how likely is it to be correct? This is the statistical concept of precision — not how much AI the tool catches, but how trustworthy it is when it says AI is present. The research on this question is unambiguous.

<1%
Document False Positive Rate
at 20%+ threshold
When Turnitin flags a document above 20%, the probability that it is flagging an innocent student is less than 1 in 100.

This figure comes directly from Turnitin's Chief Product Officer and is independently validated against 800,000 academic papers written before ChatGPT existed — a dataset large enough to treat as a reliable ground truth. The 20% threshold is not arbitrary: it is precisely the point at which Turnitin's own testing showed false positive rates dropping below 1%. Below 20%, the tool shows *% and withholds judgment. At 20% and above, it is at its most confident.

Source: Turnitin CPO Annie Chechitelli, official blog post (2023), subsequently validated in Turnitin AIW-2 White Paper (August 2024). Weber-Wulff et al. (2023, International Journal for Educational Integrity) found zero false accusations in independent testing of Turnitin. This figure refers to document-level precision — the reliability of the flag itself.

Two Different Questions — Only One Matters for Our Students
Question A — Recall
"Does Turnitin catch all AI writing?"
Answer: No. The tool intentionally misses approximately 15% of AI-written content to keep false positives low. Heavily edited or paraphrased AI text is harder to detect. Accuracy on hybrid/mixed-authorship documents ranges from 20–63%.
This is the question critics focus on. It is NOT the question relevant to determining whether our flagged students used AI.
Question B — Precision
"When Turnitin flags a student, is it right?"
Answer: Yes, with <1% error at the document level above 20%. The flag is highly reliable. A student at 35%, 48%, or 61% AI is almost certainly not a false positive. The tool errs toward missing AI, not toward falsely accusing students.
This is the question that matters for our academic integrity decisions. The answer is strongly in favor of the tool's trustworthiness.

External Research — False Positive Rates at the 20%+ Threshold

A common objection to AI detection flags is: "What if these are all false positives?" This is a legitimate question — and external research answers it directly. The concern about false positives is real at low scores. At the 20%+ threshold Turnitin specifically designed to minimize false positives, independent research consistently finds the rate to be very low. Below is what the evidence actually shows.

The most-cited alarm figure — but from a sample of only 16 documents, tested before Turnitin's major 2024 model updates and before the 20% threshold floor was introduced. It is the outlier in the literature, not the consensus.
What This Means for ASD

Across 91 papers, amnesty was offered to all students — flagged or not. The students who came forward were overwhelmingly the ones Turnitin had flagged. One unflagged student came forward admitting AI use Turnitin had missed — a confirmed false negative. Not one flagged student came forward to dispute their result. This does not prove zero false positives exist, but it strongly suggests they are rare: if a student had been wrongly accused, an anonymous, consequence-free amnesty was the perfect opportunity to say so. None did.

What Turnitin Detects (and How It's Evolving)

The 20% threshold is not arbitrary. Turnitin deliberately suppresses scores below 20% — showing *% instead — to protect students from borderline false positives. A flag of 20% or above represents Turnitin at its most confident. As of late 2025, Turnitin now runs three models simultaneously: the core AI writing detector, a paraphrase/text-spinner detector, and a newly added AI bypasser/humanizer detector. This means students who process AI text through tools like QuillBot or dedicated "humanizer" applications are increasingly detectable — the evasion gap is narrowing.

Section 02 — Internal Validation Study
We Tested It Ourselves. The Results Are Unambiguous.

Before drawing conclusions from student data, I conducted a personal validation study to test Turnitin's reliability firsthand. The goal was simple: submit documents with known authorship — some entirely my own, one entirely AI-generated — and observe what Turnitin returned.

Methodology

Group 1 — Authentic Academic Writing (6 documents): I uploaded papers I wrote during my master's degree — a mix of research papers and reflective teaching essays written before AI writing tools existed. These represent a range of academic genres: formal research argumentation, literature review, and personal pedagogical reflection. I also included a TOK essay I wrote myself as a model for students, with no AI involvement. All were written entirely by me, with no AI assistance of any kind.

Group 2 — Pre-AI Student Writing (9 documents): The 2023 AP Research cohort submitted their papers in April 2023 — just months after ChatGPT's public release, when access was still limited and its use in student writing was minimal. These papers were used as a second baseline: authentic student writing from a period before AI was embedded in academic workflows.

Group 3 — AI-Generated Writing (1 document): I wrote a TOK essay using ChatGPT, deliberately mimicking how students use it — not in a single prompt, but paragraph by paragraph, re-prompting the AI to incorporate new ideas, sharpen arguments, and improve transitions. This mirrors real-world student AI use. The final document was submitted to Turnitin to see if this method of AI use would be detected.

Group 4 — 2022 IB Extended Essays & TOK Essays (28 documents): EE and TOK essays submitted by students in 2022 — before ChatGPT existed — were run through Turnitin's current AI detection model. These are the same essay types as Cohort 14, from the same school, assessed by the same tool. They represent the most direct possible pre-ChatGPT baseline: authentic IB work by students who had no access to generative AI.

Section 05 — Qualitative Finding
The "Inception Effect": AI Influence Students Don't Recognize
🧠

When Students Don't Know They're Being Influenced

Two students who came forward during the amnesty period claimed they had only used ChatGPT for feedback — not to rewrite their essays. The teacher requested and reviewed their actual ChatGPT conversation logs. What the logs revealed was striking: ChatGPT kept rewriting their ideas in small snippets, even when students asked only for feedback. Those fragments — rephrased analysis, restructured sentences, sharpened claims — slowly migrated into the paper as the student continued working. The students had no awareness that their writing was being shaped by AI. Turnitin flagged it anyway.

What Students Said
"I only asked it for feedback, not to write for me."
What the Logs Showed
ChatGPT rewrote ideas incrementally, and students incorporated those fragments — often without realizing it.
What Turnitin Found
Still flagged. Even subtle, unintentional AI influence registers in the statistical patterns Turnitin detects.
Policy implication: Students are not always consciously cheating. AI tools are designed to be persuasive and helpful — and that helpfulness becomes authorship contamination. Our policies must address not just intentional misuse, but the structural temptation that unsupervised AI access creates.

The Limitation of the Current Response

Rewriting Words Doesn't Necessarily Restore Authorship

For students whose AI use was light — grammar correction, minor rephrasing — rewriting flagged sentences may be a proportionate response. But for students where AI contributed to the argument structure, thesis development, or analytical content, paraphrasing the output does not restore authorship. The IB assesses the quality of a student's thinking, not just their phrasing. If the ideas were generated by AI and the student transcribed them, rewriting the sentences alone does not address the underlying authenticity question. Without a supervised writing comparison, we cannot tell which situation we are dealing with — and we are currently not making that distinction.

What We Can Reasonably Conclude

We cannot say with certainty how many students in Cohort 14 have submitted work that fails IB authenticity standards. False positives exist. AI use ranges from trivial to substantive. What we can say is that the current process — flagging without follow-up, and asking a handful of students to rewrite sentences — is not sufficient to answer that question.

The IB requires that submitted work represent the student's own ideas. We have a tool that is raising concerns about a significant portion of our Cohort 14 submissions. We owe it to our students — including those who may be falsely flagged — to investigate properly rather than either dismiss the flags or act on them without due process. A supervised writing comparison gives us the evidence to do both fairly.

Whether the increase reflects more AI use, improved detection, or both — the direction is clear and the trend is concerning. What we can say with confidence: no policy change occurred between September and January, and the numbers moved sharply in the wrong direction. An environment without consequences is an environment that teaches students there are none.
Section 08 — The Gap in Our Response
Our Current Response Is Not Sufficient

The Google Docs Loophole

Our current practice: if a student says they wrote the work themselves and can show edit history in Google Docs, we accept that and close the case. This is a critical vulnerability. Google Docs revision history can be fabricated. A student can open a document, paste in AI-generated text while looking at another screen, and the edits will appear as their own typing. The edit history shows that the document was typed, not where the ideas came from. We are, in effect, accepting a loophole as evidence of integrity.

The Problem
Students can type AI-generated text while viewing it on another screen. Google Docs records keystrokes, not the source of ideas.
What We're Missing
A tool we pay thousands of dollars for is flagging students at high confidence. We dismiss those findings with no formal process, no panel, no institutional record.
The Consequence
Students learn they can use AI freely, cite Google Docs as evidence, and face no consequences. This is not a policy — it is a permission.
Section 09 — What We Need Now
Recommended Policies & Institutional Actions

The data supports urgent action across three domains: institutional governance, classroom practice, and a new response protocol for externally submitted papers.

⚖ Establish an Academic Integrity Panel
Form a standing panel — teachers, administrators, counselor — to review flagged AI cases with a consistent process
Google Docs history alone is not sufficient as evidence of authentic authorship; panel must require additional verification
Develop a tiered consequence system: first offense, second offense, IB/AP referral thresholds
Maintain confidential institutional records of flagged cases for pattern identification across years
Align consequence policy with IB Academic Integrity policy for cohesion in IB assessments
📋 Classroom-Level Policies
No major writing assignments sent home — all substantial writing completed in supervised class time
When computers are used for writing in class, all screens must be visible to the teacher at all times
Reduce unmonitored computer writing time; increase handwritten drafts, verbal conferencing, and in-class composition
Any AI tool use must be declared on submission with a description of how it was used
Turnitin submission required for all major writing grades; AI score recorded in teacher gradebook
My Proposal — A Unified Response Protocol for All Externally Submitted Papers

The following protocol is my personal recommendation based on two years of data, the amnesty study findings, and the external research reviewed in this report. Our current procedures — reflections, supervisor meetings, process journals — have not worked as a deterrent. The protocol below is designed to produce verifiable evidence of authorship, not just procedural compliance, and applies to any externally submitted paper regardless of programme. I am presenting it here for administrative consideration.

📋 Proposed Response Protocol — All Externally Submitted Papers (EE · TOK · AP Research · AP Seminar · AP Language)
Any externally submitted paper flagged above 20% AI triggers a mandatory one-hour supervised writing session — same topic, same essay type, no technology access
The supervised sample undergoes linguistic comparison against the flagged submission — vocabulary range, syntactic complexity, argumentation style, and voice consistency are analyzed
Significant divergence between the two samples escalates the case to the Academic Integrity Panel for review
The student has the right to appeal before the panel and present supporting evidence — drafts, notes, research logs, or any documentation of their writing process
If the panel finds against the student: automatic failure of the assessment — no partial credit, no resubmission for the flagged work
Consequences are more severe if a student declines the supervised write, cannot produce process evidence, or is found to have fabricated revision history
Existing programme protocols — IB reflections, supervisor meetings, process journals, AP performance task documentation — remain in place as supporting context but are no longer sufficient as standalone evidence of authorship
Build a portfolio of in-class timed writing samples early in the year to establish a linguistic baseline for each student — essential for meaningful comparison if a paper is later flagged
